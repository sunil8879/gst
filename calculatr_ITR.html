Key Terminology

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

The direct costs of producing the goods sold by a company. This includes the cost of the materials and labor directly used to create the good. It excludes indirect expenses, such as distribution costs and sales force costs.

Average Inventory

The median value of inventory throughout a specific period. Since inventory levels fluctuate, we take the starting (Opening) and ending (Closing) values and divide by two to get a representative baseline.

Real-World Example

Suppose a retail store has a COGS of ₹8,00,000. They started the year with ₹1,50,000 in stock and ended with ₹2,50,000.

Step 1 (Find Average Inventory):
(1,50,000 + 2,50,000) / 2 = ₹2,00,000

Step 2 (Find Ratio):
8,00,000 / 2,00,000 = 4.0

✅ Result: The store "cleans out" and refills its entire warehouse 4 times every year.

Strategic Impact Analysis

If ITR is LOW

A low ratio suggests weak sales or excessive inventory levels:

  • Dead Capital: Your money is "rotting" on shelves instead of being invested.
  • High Costs: You are paying more for warehouse space, insurance, and security.
  • Obsolescence: Risk of items becoming outdated, damaged, or expired increases.

If ITR is HIGH

A high ratio usually indicates strong sales and high efficiency:

  • Lean Business: You have very little capital tied up in unsold products.
  • Fresh Stock: Customers always see the newest products.
  • The Risk: If too high, you might be losing sales because items are frequently "Out of Stock."
